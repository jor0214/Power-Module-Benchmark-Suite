Power Module Layout Study

What is a Power Module?

Read this before starting. It will help you understand what you are designing.

The physical device

A power module is a compact electronic package that controls the flow of large amounts of electrical power. They are used everywhere high currents need to be switched quickly — electric vehicles, industrial motor drives, solar inverters, and railway traction systems.

The images below show a real power module (PM1000) from three angles. You can see the semiconductor devices mounted on a substrate inside the package, with copper tracks connecting them to the external terminals.

Angled view — shows the internal layout and wire connections
Front view — external terminals carry DC input and AC output
Top view — semiconductor dies and copper planes visible

What's inside

Inside every power module, semiconductor switching devices (similar to transistors) sit on a ceramic substrate. They are electrically connected using copper planes — large flat areas of copper that carry current between the devices and the external connector terminals.

Unlike the thin tracks on a regular PCB, copper planes are wide filled regions. This minimises electrical resistance and heat — critical when carrying tens or hundreds of amps.

Your task in this study is to design those copper planes. You will be given the schematic (the circuit diagram) and the fixed positions of the components. You draw the copper shapes that connect everything up correctly.

The three connections you will draw

Every layout in this study uses the same three electrical connections. Use the schematic during the task to work out which components belong to each one.

DCPDC positive — high voltage input
ACAC midpoint — the switched output
DCNDC negative — return path
Key principle: Each switching device sits physically inside the copper plane it belongs to — the electrical connection is made by the device resting on that copper. Use the schematic to decide which plane each device belongs to.

Rules for drawing

RULE 01
All pads of the same net must be joined by one continuous copper area.
RULE 02
Copper areas of different nets must not touch or overlap.
RULE 03
All copper must stay inside the board boundary (red outline).
RULE 04
Leave at least 1 grid square of clearance between adjacent planes.
RULE 05
Your polygons don't need to be neat — topological correctness matters more than shape precision.
RULE 06
For layouts with parallel devices, try to keep the plane symmetric between them.

How to use the drawing tool

  • Select the correct net colour in the left panel before drawing each shape
  • Draw plane — click to place polygon vertices; double-click or right-click to close
  • Draw route — click waypoints for a thick bondwire-style connection; double-click to finish
  • Undo removes the last vertex; Clear all resets the canvas
  • The schematic is always visible on the right — refer to it throughout
  • When happy with your design click Submit task
Timer: The drawing timer only starts when you first click on the canvas. Time spent reading this page or studying the schematic does not count. There is no time limit.

What a correct solution looks like

Key things to notice

Devices sit inside their plane. Each device's body sits entirely within the copper plane that corresponds to its drain connection — U1 sits in the DCP plane because its drain connects to DCP. The connection is made simply by the device resting on that copper.
Bondwires bridge to the next plane. The source pads on the exposed side of the device cannot make contact through the plane beneath — they need bondwires (the grey lines) arcing across to the adjacent net plane. In this layout, U1's source pad is bonded across to the AC plane.
Connector pads sit in their plane too. J1 and J2 (DCP connectors) sit inside the DCP plane. J7 and J8 (AC) sit inside the AC plane. J5 and J6 (DCN) sit inside the DCN plane.
Main task — two parallel devices per switch. The same principles apply, but now U1 and U3 are both high-side devices and must both sit within the same DCP plane. U2 and U4 are both low-side and must both sit within DCN. The AC plane connects all four devices at the midpoint. Symmetry between the parallel devices matters — try to make the current path equal length to both.

Correct layout

Key things to notice

Both parallel dies in the same plane. U1 and U3 are both high-side devices — they both sit inside the DCP plane. U2 and U4 are both low-side — they both sit inside DCN. The AC plane bridges the midpoint between all four devices.
Symmetry matters. The DCP plane reaches both U1 and U3 with roughly equal path lengths. Asymmetric planes cause one device to carry more current than the other, leading to uneven heating and reduced reliability.
Hard task coming up. The next layout uses a mirrored arrangement — the two devices face opposite directions. The connector positions may not be where you expect. Read the schematic carefully.

Correct layout

Key things to notice

Mirrored devices require careful reading. U2 (left) and U1 (right) face opposite directions — their drain and source pads are on opposite sides compared to the previous layouts. The schematic is the only reliable guide to which plane each device belongs to.
DCP and DCN connectors are on the same side. Both are on the left edge, which means the DCP plane must reach across to U1 on the right, while DCN stays local to U2 on the left. This is the non-obvious part of the layout.
The AC plane is the largest. It must bridge the midpoint between U1 source and U2 drain, connecting to the AC connectors on the right side of the board.
